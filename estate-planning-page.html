Our Approach

How we approach your estate plan

We serve as your financial quarterback — coordinating with your estate attorney and CPA to ensure every piece of your plan works together.

1

Estate Audit

We review your existing documents, account titles, beneficiary designations, and insurance policies to identify gaps, conflicts, and outdated instructions.

2

Goals & Intentions Conversation

We explore who you want to benefit, in what proportion, at what age or milestone, and what values or restrictions — if any — you want attached to an inheritance.

We recommend specific strategies — trust structures, account titling, gifting plans — and explain the tradeoffs in plain language before you commit to anything.

We work directly with your estate attorney (or refer you to one) to implement the legal documents that bring your plan to life.

Tax laws change. Families change. We review your estate plan annually and flag any updates needed to keep it aligned with your wishes and the law.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

The questions we hear most — answered without the legalese.

Do I really need a trust, or is a will enough?
+
For many people, a will is a solid foundation. But a revocable living trust can offer significant advantages — avoiding probate, providing privacy, and allowing for seamless management of assets if you become incapacitated. Whether a trust makes sense depends on your state, asset types, and family situation. We walk through this with every client.
What happens if I die without a will?
+
Your state's intestacy laws determine who inherits — and the outcome may not reflect your wishes. For example, in many states, assets are divided between a spouse and children in ways families often find surprising or problematic. A will ensures your intentions are honored.
How often should I update my estate plan?
+
We recommend reviewing your estate plan every 3–5 years, and immediately after major life events — marriage, divorce, birth of a child or grandchild, death of a beneficiary, significant change in assets, or a move to a new state. Tax law changes can also trigger the need for updates.
Are you attorneys? Do you write the legal documents?
+
We are financial advisors, not attorneys, so we do not draft legal documents. However, we play a critical coordination role — helping you understand your options, develop your strategy, and work effectively with your estate attorney to implement the plan. We can also refer you to trusted attorneys if you don't already have one.
